Why Preventive Maintenance Programs Quietly Fail

PM completion rates can look great on a KPI dashboard and still leave equipment failing on schedule. Compliance and effectiveness are not the same number.

Juan Díaz · Maintenance · 7 min read

Walk into most plants and ask how the preventive maintenance program is doing, and you'll get a number: 92% completion, maybe 95% on a good month. It sounds like a program that's working. Then you look at the breakdown log for the same period and the same motors, the same gearboxes, the same recurring faults are still showing up — on schedule, almost like clockwork.

That contradiction isn't a data problem. It's a definition problem. A completed PM task and an effective one are two different things, and most maintenance organizations only measure the first.

The compliance trap

PM completion percentage answers one question: did a technician sign off on the task within the scheduled window? It says nothing about whether the task actually addressed how that piece of equipment fails. A lubrication route can be 100% complete and still miss a bearing that's failing from misalignment, not lack of grease. An inspection checklist can be fully signed off by someone who was never taught what a developing fault actually looks or sounds like.

Completion rate is easy to measure, so it becomes the metric everyone reports up the chain. Task quality is harder to measure, so it quietly stops being asked about. Over time, the program optimizes for the number that's visible instead of the outcome that matters.

A PM task that doesn't match the equipment's actual failure mode isn't preventing anything — it's just paperwork with a due date.

Where the tasks themselves go wrong

Most legacy PM programs were built once, years ago, often copied from an OEM manual or a previous plant, and never revised against what actually breaks. That creates a few predictable failure patterns:

  • Time-based tasks on equipment that fails on condition, not time. Changing a component every 90 days regardless of how it's actually wearing either wastes good parts or misses a failure that develops faster than the interval.
  • Generic tasks that don't reflect the specific failure mode. "Inspect motor" isn't a task — it doesn't tell a technician what a bad bearing sounds like versus a winding problem versus a coupling issue.
  • No feedback loop from breakdowns back into the PM list. When a failure happens, the PM program should get updated. In most plants, the breakdown gets fixed and the PM list stays exactly as it was.
  • Task overload. When technicians are handed more PM tasks than there's time to do properly, quality quietly drops first, and completion rate becomes the only thing that survives.

What effective PM actually requires

Fixing this isn't about doing more PM — it's about making sure the PM that exists is pointed at real failure modes. A few practices consistently separate programs that reduce failures from programs that just generate paperwork:

Build tasks from failure modes, not manufacturer defaults

An FMEA — even an informal one built from a maintenance team's own history — tells you how a piece of equipment actually fails on your line, under your conditions. That's a better starting point for a PM task than a generic OEM interval, which is usually written to avoid warranty claims, not to match your operating environment.

Close the loop after every failure

Every unplanned breakdown is a data point about a gap in the PM program — either a missing task, a wrong interval, or a task that isn't specific enough to catch what actually happened. If that breakdown doesn't change the PM list, the same gap will produce the same failure again.

Add condition-based checks where they earn their cost

Not everything needs vibration analysis or thermography. But for the assets that drive the most downtime, condition monitoring catches developing faults that a fixed-interval inspection will walk past. The goal isn't to monitor everything — it's to monitor what actually matters and use time-based PM everywhere else.

Audit task quality, not just task completion

Occasionally follow a technician through a PM route and ask what they're actually checking and why. It surfaces two things fast: tasks that no longer make sense for the equipment as installed, and technicians who were never properly trained on what the task is supposed to catch.

The number that actually matters

Completion rate isn't a bad metric — it's an incomplete one. The plants that get real reliability improvement out of their PM program track a second number alongside it: how many of the breakdowns that happened this month were on equipment covered by a PM task that should have caught the failure mode. That number is uncomfortable to report. It's also the one that tells you whether the program is actually working.
